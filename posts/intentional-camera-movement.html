On Intentional Camera Movement: Surrendering Control

There is a particular kind of terror in letting go of a photograph mid-exposure. ICM asks you to unlearn everything you know about holding still. The wrist rotation that destroys a landscape becomes the gesture that saves it.

I've been practising for six years and I still don't fully know what I'm doing — and that's exactly the point. The moment you think you've mastered it, the results start feeling mechanical. ICM requires a commitment to genuine uncertainty.

The mechanics

A shutter speed between 1/4 and 2 seconds is where most of the interesting work happens. Too fast and the blur is too subtle. Too slow and the image dissolves into abstraction — which can be beautiful, but it's a different discipline.

The camera is just a brush. The sensor is just a canvas. The motion is the paint.

I hold the camera loosely — no death grip, no locked wrists. I let the weight of the lens do part of the work. For vertical blurs like forest trees, a slow upward motion with a slight spiral at the end. For horizontal urban blurs, a side-to-side motion with a late acceleration.

What you can't control

Wind. Your own heartbeat at 1/2 second. The subject moving independently of your motion. These are features, not bugs. The images that surprise me most are always the ones where something moved that I didn't intend.
