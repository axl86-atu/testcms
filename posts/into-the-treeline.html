Into the Treeline

The figure was a stranger. By the time I had the camera set up they were almost gone. I pressed the shutter anyway and got three seconds of someone disappearing into fog.

I was in the Schwarzwald at 5:45am in November with frost on the ground and no particular reason to be there except that I had read somewhere that the fog in that forest at that time of year does something specific to the light between the trees. The person who told me that was right.

The figure appeared from nowhere. A walker, early, moving away from me down the path at a pace that suggested they had somewhere to be or were trying to outrun something. I had three seconds before the fog took them.

I got the shot. They never knew I was there.

"It is not enough to have talent. You also have to be Hungarian." — Robert Capa

I think about that a lot. Talent is preparation. Being there at 5:45am in November is the other half.

On fog

Fog is the photographer's most cooperative collaborator and most unreliable one. It arrives when it wants to and leaves the same way. It changes the quality of light in a forest from something harsh and directional into something ambient and sourceless — light that seems to come from everywhere and nowhere, that makes the trees look like they are glowing from inside rather than being lit from outside.

The three-second exposure turned the fog into a presence rather than a condition. It is not weather in this photograph. It is substance. The figure is walking into something solid.
