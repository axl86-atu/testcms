One Face, Six Times

I asked the same person to sit still for six exposures on the same frame. They moved anyway. That was the point.

The instruction was simple: sit in the same position for six exposures, two seconds each, with a ten second gap between them. Don't try to hold still. Just sit.

Nobody sits still for ten seconds. The spine shifts. The head drops a fraction. The eyes move even when the face doesn't. Six exposures of the same person in the same chair and what you get is not a portrait — it is an argument between six versions of the same face about which one is the real one. None of them win.

On identity and the long exposure

"We are all ghosts of ourselves." — Ralph Eugene Meatyard

Portrait photography has always lied. The single frozen frame implies a self that is stable, readable, fixed. This is the fundamental fiction of the medium — that a person can be captured. They cannot. You can only capture a slice of light reflected from a surface at a specific moment and call it a person if you want to.

The multiple exposure makes the lie visible. Here are six slices. Here is the instability they've been hiding.

What the camera actually saw

A forehead in six slightly different positions stacked into something that looks like a crown. Eyes that exist in triplicate across a single face. A jaw that has become a smear of silver. The sitter told me afterwards they felt watched. I told them they were right — watched six times simultaneously, which is different from being watched once.

"To photograph people is to violate them." — Susan Sontag

I don't entirely agree with Sontag here but I understand why she said it. There is something aggressive about this particular image. The repetition feels less like a portrait and more like an interrogation — the same question asked six times, hoping the answer will change. It didn't. That was the other point.

Process note

Shot on a Mamiya RB67. 120mm film, Ilford Delta 400 pushed to 1600. Six two-second exposures on a single frame with the dark slide partially reinserted between each one to prevent fogging. Printed on fibre, grade 4 filter, significant burning in the edges.
