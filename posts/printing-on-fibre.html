Printing on Fibre: A Slower Practice

Resin-coated paper dries in minutes. Fibre takes hours. That difference in time changes how you think.

Resin-coated paper dries in minutes. Fibre takes hours. That difference in time changes how you think about a print.

With RC paper, you can run a test strip, adjust, and have a finished print in twenty minutes. With fibre, the drying, flattening, and washing alone take the better part of a day. You make fewer prints. You consider each one more carefully.

Why bother

Permanence. A well-processed fibre print, properly fixed and washed, will outlast any inkjet or digital print made today. Archivally speaking, we're talking centuries versus decades. For work that matters, that matters.

There's also the surface. Fibre paper has a depth that RC can't replicate — the highlights have a warmth, the shadows have a density. Under a loupe you can see the silver in the emulsion. You're looking at actual silver, not a dye simulation of it.

Every fibre print is an object. An RC print is a document.

My process: expose, develop in Ilford Multigrade, stop, fix, wash for ten minutes, tone in selenium 1:20 for four minutes, wash for forty minutes, dry face-down on a mesh rack overnight. No shortcuts.
