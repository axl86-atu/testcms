Why I Stopped Shooting Digital Entirely

The decision took three years to make and about ten minutes to commit to. Film forces decisions. Digital defers them.

The decision took three years to make and about ten minutes to commit to. I'd been shooting hybrid — film for personal work, digital for commissions — and the gap between the two was becoming embarrassing. Not in quality. In intention.

Digital is a medium of infinite takes. Film is a medium of considered ones. My digital work was technically fine and completely bloodless. My film work had accidents in it, and the accidents were the best parts.

The cost of infinite storage

When storage is free, editing is deferred. I would come home from a shoot with 800 RAW files and spend a week in Lightroom making choices I should have made in the field. Film made me make those choices before I pressed the shutter.

You can't bracket your way to a good photograph. You can only decide.

I'm not arguing that film is objectively better. I'm arguing that the constraints of film made me a better photographer. The 36-exposure limit. The cost per frame. The wait for development. All of it forces clarity.
