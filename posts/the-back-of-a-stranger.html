The Back of a Stranger

Every great street photograph is a question that never gets answered. The back of a stranger walking away is the purest version of that question.

I have never been interested in faces. Faces tell you too much — emotion, age, intention. The back of a person tells you nothing, and nothing is where the imagination starts working. This frame was made on Langstrasse at 11pm on a Tuesday. One second exposure. The subject never knew I was there.

"The photographer is an armed version of the solitary walker." — Susan Sontag

The blur here is not a technical accident. It is the point. What you are looking at is not a person — it is the time it took for a person to cross a patch of light. Twelve steps compressed into a single silver smear.

"To photograph is to confer importance." — Susan Sontag

I keep coming back to the coat. Even dissolved into streaks you can read the weight of it, the way it moves differently than the legs beneath it. The material world survives the motion. The person does not.

That feels true of a lot of things.
